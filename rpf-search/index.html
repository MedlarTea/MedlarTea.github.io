RPF-Search addresses the challenges of person search in unknown, dynamic environments. The proposed approach enables the system to search for the target person in promising directions, even under topographic occlusion, as demonstrated in (a), despite a rough trajectory prediction. Additionally, the proposed method intelligently adapts to dynamic occlusions, either overtaking the occluders (b) or following them (c), based on occluders' motion patterns.

Abstract

Autonomous robot person-following (RPF) systems are crucial for personal assistance and security but struggle when the target is lost due to occlusions in dynamic, unknown environments. Current methods rely on pre-built maps and static assumptions, limiting effectiveness in real-world settings. This highlights a critical gap in re-identifying targets of robot person-following (RPF) systems under topographic (e.g., walls, corners) and dynamic (e.g., moving pedestrians) occlusions. In this paper, we propose a novel heuristic-guided search framework that dynamically builds environmental maps while tracking the target and adapts to various occlusions by prioritizing high-probability areas for locating the target. For topographic occlusions, a belief-guided search field evaluates the likelihood of the target's presence, while for dynamic occlusions, a fluid-field approach allows the robot to adaptively follow or overtake moving occluders. Historical motion cues and environmental observations refine the search over time. Results show our method outperforms six existing approaches, improving search efficiency and success rates in simulations and real-world tests. This enhances RPF systems' adaptability and reliability in dynamic environments, advancing real-world applications.

The framework of our field-based heuristic-guided RPF-search. The search process is divided into two categories: topographic occlusion from environmental corners and dynamic occlusion caused by moving pedestrians. For topographic occlusion, we use a belief-guided search field to optimize target search by selecting promising searching points based on information gain, target probability, motion efficiency, and collision risk. For dynamic occlusion, motion cues from pedestrians guide whether to follow via a fluid-field approach or overtake using an observation-based potential field.
